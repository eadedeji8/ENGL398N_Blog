One Year Later: How the SCD Transition Pathway Is Breaking Barriers for Young Adults

Celebrating success as our innovative program transforms care for young adults with sickle cell disease
By Emmanuel Adedeji, Program Director

One year ago, CNH launched the SCD Transition Pathway program with a singular mission: to transform how young adults with sickle cell disease move from pediatric to adult healthcare. Today, we're proud to share that what began as an ambitious idea has become a life-changing reality for dozens of young adults in the Washington, DC area.

The journey from pediatric to adult healthcare has historically been a precarious cliff for young adults with sickle cell disease. Many found themselves suddenly navigating complex medical systems without the support structures they'd grown accustomed to. The statistics were alarming: 63% experienced gaps in care, emergency visits spiked by 47%, and medication adherence plummeted.

A Comprehensive Solution Takes Shape

With generous funding secured in May 2025, our team immediately began assembling the infrastructure needed to bridge this critical gap. We hired Tasha Johnson, MSW, as our dedicated Transition Navigator, developed educational materials specifically tailored to young adults, and established formal partnerships with adult care providers throughout the region.

"The SCD Transition Pathway isn't just a program—it's a lifeline for young adults who've spent their entire lives in pediatric care and suddenly face the daunting adult healthcare system. We're not just transferring medical records; we're transferring trust, confidence, and continuity."
— Dr. Vanessa Williams, Medical Director

The Six Pillars of Success

What makes our program effective is its comprehensive approach. Rather than addressing just one aspect of transition, we've implemented six integrated components:

1. Structured Timeline: Beginning at age 16, patients follow a clear roadmap with specific milestones and checkpoints, eliminating uncertainty about what comes next.

2. Dedicated Navigator: Our Transition Navigator serves as a consistent guide throughout the entire process, helping patients overcome obstacles and connecting them with resources.

3. Self-Management Training: Through hands-on workshops and digital resources, young adults learn to manage medications, recognize emergency symptoms, and advocate for themselves in healthcare settings.

4. Peer Mentorship: Perhaps the most powerful element has been connecting transitioning patients with those who have successfully made the journey to adult care. These lived experiences provide both practical guidance and emotional support.

5. Mental Health Support: Integrated psychological services address the anxiety and stress that often accompany major life transitions.

6. Adult Provider Network: We've cultivated relationships with adult hematologists and primary care providers who understand sickle cell disease, ensuring patients have access to knowledgeable care after transition.

Overcoming Challenges

The path hasn't been without obstacles. Initially, we struggled with limited adult provider options in certain neighborhoods, leading to transportation barriers for some participants. In response, we expanded our telehealth capabilities and established transportation assistance for critical appointments.

Additionally, the peer mentorship component required more structure than originally anticipated. We developed formal training for mentors and implemented clear guidelines for the mentorship relationship, resulting in stronger connections and more consistent support.

Looking Ahead: Expansion and Innovation

Building on our first-year success, we're excited to announce three new initiatives for the coming year:

SCD Transition App: A smartphone application that will help patients track appointments, medications, and symptoms while providing direct communication with their transition navigator.

Family Support Network: Recognizing that transition affects the entire family, we're expanding resources to help parents adjust to their evolving role in their child's healthcare.

Transition Model Toolkit: We're developing comprehensive materials to help other hospitals implement similar programs for sickle cell disease and other chronic conditions.
